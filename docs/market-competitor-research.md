# WotC/d20 TTRPG Market & Competitor Research (March 2026)

## Scope and method
Focus: products used to run or manage tabletop RPG campaigns in the Wizards of the Coast (D&D) + d20 ecosystem (digital and physical). Sources include official product pages plus player/GM discussion threads (Reddit, community docs, GM prep resources).

## Competitor landscape

### Software products (direct and adjacent)
- **D&D Beyond (official WotC stack)**: Character builder, campaigns, encounters, game log, and official Maps VTT positioning around "prep less, play more." Strongest in official rules/content integration and onboarding. [1]
- **Roll20**: Browser-first VTT with free tier, large licensed library, dynamic lighting/fog, campaign organization, and integrated sheets/dice. Strongest in accessibility and low setup friction. [2]
- **Foundry VTT**: One-time purchase, self-hosted ownership, heavy modular extensibility via API/modules. Strongest for power users and customization. [3]
- **Fantasy Grounds**: Automation-heavy VTT with large licensed catalog and free-to-play entry path. Strongest in deep rules automation + marketplace depth. [4]
- **Owlbear Rodeo**: Lightweight room/scene model, fog tooling, and extension ecosystem (including verified extensions, third-party initiative/combat tooling). Strongest in speed/simplicity for map-centric play. [5]
- **World Anvil (adjacent campaign manager)**: Worldbuilding templates, timelines, maps, statblocks, and campaign management. Strongest in lore/wiki and long-form campaign knowledge management. [6]
- **Open5e (ecosystem utility)**: Open SRD API/content access for d20-compatible tools. Strongest for builders/integrators around open rules content. [7]

### Physical products (campaign operations layer)
- **The Rook & The Raven**: 5e-specific DM and player planners/journals with structured pages for encounters, arcs, NPCs, session notes. [8]
- **Loke BattleMats**: Modular dry-erase map books, GM screens, encounter/randomization accessories, and VTT-ready digital map bundles. [9]
- **Other notebook/planner publishers** (example: Raven and the Goose): 5e DM notebooks emphasizing campaign outline, NPC/monster tracking, and quick rules references. [10]

## Common features across multiple products
- Character and party state tracking.
- Encounter/combat management (initiative, HP/status, tactical maps).
- Campaign knowledge organization (NPCs, quests, locations, notes, timelines).
- Rules/content lookup and reference acceleration.
- Session prep acceleration (templates, reusable assets, map/token packs, random tables).
- Collaboration and sharing (party access, content sharing, invites, cloud sync/community spaces).
- Hybrid play support (in-person + online + async notes).

## Core use cases these products solve
- **Run a session reliably**: less table downtime, faster combat turns, easier adjudication.
- **Reduce prep burden**: reusable maps/encounters, lightweight planning frameworks, faster recall.
- **Maintain campaign continuity**: preserve canon/lore and character history over long arcs.
- **Coordinate distributed groups**: online play, scheduling, remote player participation.
- **Lower new-DM/new-player friction**: guided workflows and ready-to-run content.

## Known gaps and recurring pain points (from community discussions)
- **Prep overhead remains high**, especially VTT map/lighting/asset setup; many DMs report burnout risk. [11]
- **Complexity cliffs**: advanced VTTs offer power but impose learning/configuration cost. [12]
- **Performance variance by hardware/module load**: richer visual stacks can punish older devices. [12]
- **Fragmented toolchain**: many groups stitch together multiple apps (VTT + notes + Discord + music + extensions). [13]
- **Content access/licensing friction**: recurring community demand for shared access to paid libraries suggests cost/ownership pressure points. [14]
- **Physical product reliability risk**: some community threads raise fulfillment concerns for boutique accessories, indicating trust/logistics risk in non-digital channels. [15]

Inference from sources: the “winner” is often not the most feature-rich platform, but the one with the best balance of **prep-time reduction + reliability + cognitive simplicity** for a specific table.

## Value drivers to prioritize (players and GMs)
- **Story momentum**: fewer operational interruptions, better narrative flow.
- **Creative expression**: worldbuilding, homebrew support, and flexible storytelling tools.
- **Tactical clarity**: fast combat state visibility and reduced rules ambiguity.
- **Group cohesion**: shared memory of events, lore, and character relationships.
- **Immersion**: maps, fog, handouts, audio/visual cues, and discoverability of secrets.
- **Agency and choice**: tools that react to player decisions without over-constraining GM improvisation.
- **Time efficiency**: high session quality per hour of prep.
- **Control/ownership** (especially for GMs): data portability, fewer lock-in concerns, predictable costs.

## Product strategy implications for this repo
- Start with a **low-friction campaign ops core**: notes, NPC/quest tracker, session timeline, and lightweight encounter board.
- Design for **hybrid mode first** (table + browser), not VTT-only.
- Add **progressive complexity**: “works in 10 minutes” default, advanced automation optional.
- Treat **prep-time savings** as the primary KPI (minutes saved per session).
